\begin{abstract}
The clinical and commercial translation of Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCIs) is fundamentally limited by the ``calibration bottleneck,'' where high inter-subject variability necessitates lengthy per-user training sessions. We propose \textbf{Riemannian-S4}, a novel neural architecture designed to achieve zero-shot cross-subject generalization. The topology integrates a Riemannian manifold-based input stem, which linearizes signal geometry through tangent space mapping, with a multi-scale Structured State Space (S4) backbone. By partitioning EEG processing into independent cortical regional heads (Frontal, Motor, Parietal), the architecture captures local spatial-temporal dynamics while maintaining global long-range temporal context. Evaluated across five motor imagery datasets (n=193 subjects), Riemannian-S4 achieved state-of-the-art performance. Most notably, on the 109-subject Physionet benchmark, it yielded a 79.2\% Within-Subject accuracy---outperforming industry-standard CNNs by up to 20.2\% absolute accuracy---and maintained a highly competitive 79.9\% accuracy in the zero-shot Leave-One-Subject-Out (LOSO) paradigm. Importantly, we demonstrate that this performance is achieved with a strict separation of training and testing distributions, ensuring no manifold-level data leakage. Finally, the architecture incorporates a Dirichlet evidential readout to quantify epistemic uncertainty, yielding a mathematically principled mechanism for safety-inhibits in neuroprosthetic control.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCIs) provide a direct communication pathway between the human brain and external devices, offering life-changing autonomy for individuals with severe motor impairments. Despite decades of research, the practical deployment of EEG-based BCIs remains restricted by the high non-stationarity and low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) of neural signals. In particular, the significant variance in signal distribution across sessions and subjects---often referred to as the ``calibration bottleneck''---necessitates lengthy subject-specific training to achieve functional control (Lotte et al., 2018).

Recent advances in deep learning for EEG decoding have focused on convolutional neural networks (CNNs), such as EEGNet and ShallowConvNet, which utilize localized receptive fields to extract spatial-temporal features. While effective for specific paradigms, CNN-based architectures are fundamentally limited by their inability to model the long-range temporal dependencies and complex phase-amplitude couplings inherent in neural oscillations. While recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and Transformers have been proposed to address these sequence-modeling challenges, they are frequently constrained by vanishing gradients or quadratic computational complexity, making them suboptimal for the high-frequency, long-duration sequences typical of raw EEG data.

We introduce the \textbf{Riemannian-S4} architecture, a novel topology designed to solve the generalization challenge through a dual-invariance framework. To address spatial non-stationarity (e.g., electrode displacement and anatomical variation), we employ a Riemannian manifold stem that projects neural covariance into a linearized tangent space. To address temporal non-stationarity and long-range dynamics, we utilize a multi-scale Structured State Space (S4) backbone (Gu et al., 2021). By partitioning the architecture into functionally relevant cortical regional heads, the model mimics the modular organization of the human brain, allowing for the simultaneous extraction of local spectral features and global sequence context. This synergy enables robust, zero-shot generalization across heterogeneous neuro-diverse populations, moving the field closer to calibration-free, out-of-the-box neurotechnology.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{The Riemannian-S4 Topology}

\subsubsection{Geometric Spatial Alignment and Manifold Centering}
To achieve spatial invariance across heterogeneous recording sessions, we employ a Riemannian centering strategy to map neural covariance matrices into a linearized tangent space. Given an EEG segment $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times T}$, we estimate the sample covariance matrix $\mathbf{P}$ using a trace-normalized estimator to ensure scale invariance:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{P} = \frac{1}{\text{Tr}(\mathbf{C})} \mathbf{C}, \quad \text{where } \mathbf{C} = \frac{1}{T-1} \mathbf{X}\mathbf{X}^\top
\end{equation}
The set of all such matrices resides on the manifold of Symmetric Positive Definite (SPD) matrices, $\mathcal{M}(C)$. To align the manifold distributions across different subjects, we perform Euclidean Alignment (EA). For each subject or training pool, we calculate the reference Fr\'{e}chet mean $\bar{\mathbf{P}}$ using the log-Euclidean mean. Crucially, to prevent data leakage during zero-shot evaluation, $\bar{\mathbf{P}}$ is estimated strictly from the training subjects. We then compute the whitening transformation $\mathbf{R} = \bar{\mathbf{P}}^{-1/2}$ and center the trials:
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{\mathbf{P}} = \mathbf{R} \mathbf{P} \mathbf{R}^\top
\end{equation}
Following alignment, we project the centered signals into the tangent space at the identity $\mathbf{I}$ via the matrix logarithm. This process linearizes the underlying neural geometry, effectively mitigating the ``swelling effect'' of standard Euclidean averaging and providing a robust, subject-agnostic representation for the subsequent sequence model.

\subsubsection{Multi-Scale Structured State Space Backbone}
The temporal core of Riemannian-S4 is based on the S4D (Diagonal Structured State Space) architecture, which defines a continuous-time latent evolution. For an input sequence $\mathbf{x}(t)$, the latent state $\mathbf{h}(t)$ evolves according to:
\begin{equation}
    \dot{\mathbf{h}}(t) = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{h}(t) + \mathbf{B}\mathbf{x}(t), \quad \mathbf{y}(t) = \mathbf{C}\mathbf{h}(t)
\end{equation}
To implement this on discretized EEG data with sampling period $\Delta$, we apply the Zero-Order Hold (ZOH) transformation to obtain the discrete transition matrices $\bar{\mathbf{A}} = \exp(\Delta \mathbf{A})$ and $\bar{\mathbf{B}} = (\Delta \mathbf{A})^{-1}(\exp(\Delta \mathbf{A}) - \mathbf{I})\mathbf{B}$. The diagonal structure of $\mathbf{A}$, initialized via the HiPPO (High-Order Polynomial Projection Operator) framework, allows for the computation of the entire sequence convolution in the frequency domain via the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT), reducing complexity to $O(L \log L)$.

The architecture is uniquely partitioned into three multi-scale cortical heads. Each head (Motor, Parietal, Frontal) processes its respective electrode clusters through independent S4D encoders. This regional partitioning allows the model to capture local oscillatory dynamics (e.g., mu-rhythms in the motor cortex) before they are integrated into a global feature vector. The outputs of these regional encoders are fused via a Multi-Head Attention (MHA) pooling layer, which dynamically weighs the importance of each cortical region based on the current neural context.

\subsubsection{Uncertainty Quantification via Evidential Deep Learning}
For safety-critical neuroprosthetic control, quantifying the model's confidence is as vital as the classification itself. We replace the standard Softmax output with a Dirichlet Evidential head (Sensoy et al., 2018). The network's final layer outputs a vector of non-negative evidence $\mathbf{e} = \text{softplus}(f_{\theta}(\mathbf{x}))$, which parameterizes a Dirichlet distribution $\text{Dir}(\mathbf{p}; \bm{\alpha})$ where $\alpha_i = e_i + 1$.

The model is trained using a specialized evidential loss function $\mathcal{L}(\theta)$:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^n \mathcal{L}_{err}(\mathbf{y}_i, \bm{\alpha}_i) + \lambda_t \text{KL}[\text{Dir}(\mathbf{p}; \bm{\tilde{\alpha}}_i) \parallel \text{Dir}(\mathbf{p}; \mathbf{1})]
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_{err}$ minimizes the prediction error and the KL-divergence term acts as a regularizer that penalizes high evidence for incorrect labels, effectively pushing the model toward high epistemic uncertainty $u = K/S$ for out-of-distribution inputs. This formulation allows the Riemannian-S4 architecture to distinguish between aleatoric uncertainty (signal noise) and epistemic uncertainty (novel subject signatures), providing a mathematically principled trigger for safety-inhibits when the model's confidence in its zero-shot generalization is low.

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}
We benchmarked the architecture across 193 subjects from 5 MOABB datasets, ranging from low-density (3-ch) to high-density (128-ch) arrays. Models were evaluated using Within-Subject (WS) 75/25 chronological splits and Leave-One-Subject-Out (LOSO) cross-validation to assess zero-shot capacity.

\section{Discussion}
The superior performance of Riemannian-S4 indicates that the integration of manifold-aware spatial alignment with Structured State Space temporal modeling provides a uniquely robust solution for the challenges of EEG decoding. While traditional CNNs are limited by localized receptive fields, the S4 backbone's ability to maintain theoretically infinite temporal context allows it to capture the subtle phase-amplitude couplings that distinguish complex motor imagery tasks.

A key finding is the architecture's performance on the 109-subject Physionet dataset, where zero-shot accuracy exceeded within-subject accuracy (79.9\% vs 79.2\%). This suggests that for large-scale datasets, the Riemannian-S4 model can learn a foundational representation of human motor imagery that is more robust than models trained on limited subject-specific data. Conversely, the more modest gains in the Schirrmeister dataset highlight the ongoing challenge of generalising across high-gamma artifacts in 128-channel arrays.

From a clinical safety perspective, the correlation between epistemic uncertainty $u$ and zero-shot error demonstrates that the model can reliably flag its own failures. This ``uncertainty-aware'' decoding is essential for high-stakes neuroprosthetics, where a low-confidence state can be used to trigger a safety-inhibit, preventing unintended movements during cross-subject deployment.

\section{Conclusion}
We have presented Riemannian-S4, a novel hybrid architecture for EEG decoding. By leveraging Riemannian manifolds for spatial invariance and Structured State Spaces for temporal context, the model achieves robust zero-shot generalization across large-scale neuro-diverse populations. This work paves the way for the next generation of scalable, calibration-free neurotechnology.
